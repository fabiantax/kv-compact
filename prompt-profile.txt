Explain in detail the history of computer science, starting from the earliest mechanical computing devices through modern artificial intelligence. Cover the following topics thoroughly:

Charles Babbage designed the Analytical Engine in the 1830s, which is considered the first general-purpose computer concept. Ada Lovelace wrote what is recognized as the first computer program for this machine. The Analytical Engine included concepts that would later become fundamental to computing: an arithmetic logic unit, control flow through conditional branching and loops, and integrated memory. Babbage's difference engine, designed to compute polynomial functions, was a simpler predecessor.

Alan Turing published his seminal paper "On Computable Numbers" in 1936, introducing the concept of the Turing machine. This abstract mathematical model defined the limits of what can be computed and laid the theoretical foundation for computer science. Turing's work at Bletchley Park during World War II, breaking the Enigma cipher, demonstrated practical applications of computational thinking. The Turing test, proposed in 1950, remains a benchmark for artificial intelligence.

The ENIAC, completed in 1945, was the first electronic general-purpose digital computer. It weighed 30 tons, occupied 1,800 square feet, and consumed 150 kilowatts of power. Despite these enormous physical requirements, its computational power was less than that of a modern calculator. The von Neumann architecture, proposed around the same time, introduced the stored-program concept where instructions and data share the same memory space.

The invention of the transistor at Bell Labs in 1947 revolutionized electronics and computing. Transistors replaced vacuum tubes, making computers smaller, faster, more reliable, and less expensive. The integrated circuit, developed independently by Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce in 1958-1959, placed multiple transistors on a single chip, further miniaturizing computing hardware.

The development of programming languages progressed from machine code to assembly language to high-level languages. FORTRAN, developed by IBM in 1957, was the first widely used high-level programming language. LISP, created by John McCarthy in 1958, introduced many concepts fundamental to modern programming including garbage collection, tree data structures, and dynamic typing. COBOL, designed in 1959, became the dominant language for business applications.

The 1960s and 1970s saw the development of operating systems, networking protocols, and database systems. Unix, created at Bell Labs in 1969, introduced a modular design philosophy that influenced all subsequent operating systems. The C programming language, developed alongside Unix by Dennis Ritchie, became one of the most influential programming languages ever created. The ARPANET, predecessor to the Internet, was established in 1969.

The personal computer revolution began in the late 1970s with machines like the Apple II, Commodore PET, and TRS-80. IBM's entry into the personal computer market in 1981 established the PC standard. The graphical user interface, developed at Xerox PARC and popularized by Apple's Macintosh in 1984, fundamentally changed how humans interact with computers.

The World Wide Web, invented by Tim Berners-Lee at CERN in 1989, transformed the Internet from a research tool into a global communication platform. HTML, HTTP, and URLs provided the foundation for the web as we know it. The development of web browsers, starting with Mosaic in 1993 and Netscape Navigator in 1994, made the web accessible to the general public.

The rise of machine learning and artificial intelligence in the 21st century represents the latest chapter in this history. Deep learning, powered by neural networks with many layers, has achieved remarkable results in image recognition, natural language processing, and game playing. The transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, revolutionized natural language processing and led to large language models like GPT, BERT, and their successors.

Modern AI systems can generate human-like text, create images from descriptions, write computer code, and engage in complex reasoning. The development of attention mechanisms, particularly self-attention, enabled models to process long sequences efficiently by allowing each element to attend to all others. Key-value caching during inference stores previously computed attention states, trading memory for computation speed.
